What is PrEP?

Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis is a powerful tool in preventing HIV

Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis (PrEP) is a medication taken by HIV-negative people to reduce their risk of HIV infection. When taken as prescribed, PrEP is highly effective at preventing HIV from sex or injection drug use.

PrEP contains the same medicines used to treat HIV. These medicines work by stopping HIV from taking hold and spreading throughout your body.

The regimen used for oral PrEP in Botswana is TDF 300mg / FTC 200mg given as a fixed-dose combination (FDC), one tablet, once daily.

How effective is PrEP?

When taken as prescribed, PrEP reduces the risk of getting HIV from sex by about 99%. Among people who inject drugs, PrEP reduces the risk of getting HIV by at least 74% when taken consistently.

PrEP is even more effective when combined with other prevention methods like condoms and regular testing.

Who is Eligible for PrEP?

PrEP is recommended for people who are at high risk of HIV infection

Discordant Couples

People in relationships where one partner has HIV and the other does not.

MSM

Men who have sex with men who may be at higher risk of HIV exposure.

Sex Workers

Individuals who engage in sex work and may have multiple partners.

Transgender People

Transgender individuals who may face increased risk factors.

People Who Inject Drugs

Those who share needles or injection equipment.

Adolescent Girls & Young Women

Young women in areas with high HIV prevalence who may be at risk.

How to Access PrEP in Botswana

PrEP is available through various healthcare facilities across the country

1

Consult a Healthcare Provider

Visit a PrEP-providing facility to speak with a healthcare professional about your risk factors and whether PrEP is right for you.

2

Get Tested for HIV

You'll need a negative HIV test before starting PrEP. The facility will provide this test for free.

3

Additional Health Checks

Your healthcare provider will check your kidney function and for other STIs before prescribing PrEP.

4

Receive Your PrEP Medication

If approved, you'll receive your PrEP medication free of charge through Botswana's public health system.

5

Follow-Up Visits

Regular follow-up visits every 3 months are important to monitor your health and renew your prescription.

Side Effects & Management

Understanding potential side effects and how to manage them

Most people who take PrEP do not experience significant side effects. When side effects do occur, they're usually mild and go away within the first month of taking PrEP.

Common Side Effects

  • Start-up Syndrome

    Some people experience nausea, headaches, or stomach discomfort when first starting PrEP. These symptoms typically resolve within a few weeks.

  • Kidney Function

    PrEP can affect kidney function in rare cases. Your healthcare provider will monitor this through regular tests.

  • Bone Density

    Minor decreases in bone density have been observed in some studies, but these changes are generally small and not clinically significant.

Management Tips

Take with Food

Taking PrEP with a meal can help reduce nausea and stomach discomfort.

Consistent Schedule

Taking PrEP at the same time each day can help your body adjust and maintain consistent drug levels.

Stay Hydrated

Drinking plenty of water can help minimize some side effects and support kidney health.

Regular Check-ups

Keep all follow-up appointments to monitor for any potential side effects.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about PrEP and HIV prevention

No, PrEP only protects against HIV. It does not protect against other sexually transmitted infections (STIs) like gonorrhea, chlamydia, or syphilis, nor does it prevent pregnancy. Condoms are still recommended to protect against other STIs and as a form of birth control.

For receptive anal sex, PrEP reaches maximum protection after about 7 days of daily use. For receptive vaginal sex and injection drug use, PrEP reaches maximum protection after about 21 days of daily use. It's important to continue using other prevention methods like condoms during this initial period.

Yes, PrEP is not a lifelong medication. It's meant to be used during periods when you are at risk of HIV infection. If your risk factors change, you can discuss stopping PrEP with your healthcare provider. It's important to continue taking PrEP for 28 days after your last potential exposure to HIV.

In Botswana, PrEP is provided free of charge through the public healthcare system. This includes the medication itself and the necessary follow-up testing and consultations. Private healthcare facilities may charge for consultations, but the medication is typically supplied through the government program.

Yes, PrEP can be used during pregnancy and breastfeeding. Studies have not found significant health concerns for pregnant or breastfeeding women taking PrEP or for their babies. If you're pregnant or breastfeeding and at risk for HIV, discuss PrEP with your healthcare provider.

Your PrEP use is confidential medical information. Healthcare providers are bound by confidentiality rules and cannot disclose your medical information without your permission. The PrEP pill bottle doesn't specifically indicate it's for HIV prevention, and you can always transfer pills to a different container for privacy.

If you miss a dose, take it as soon as you remember. If it's almost time for your next dose, just take the next dose at the regular time. Don't take a double dose to make up for a missed one. Missing an occasional dose is not likely to reduce PrEP's effectiveness, but trying to take it consistently is important for maximum protection.
